Electric Current and Ohm's Law

Electric current is the rate at which charge flows through a surface:

If a charge QQQ flows through in a time interval ttt, the average current is:

Iavg=Qt.I_{avg} = \frac{Q}{t}.Iavg​=tQ​.

In the limit t→0t \to 0t→0, the instantaneous current is equal to:

I=Qt.I = \frac{Q}{t}.I=tQ​.

The unit of electric current is ampere (AAA).

Current Density 

Suppose a cylindrical conductor with cross sectional area AAA with nnn charges per unit volume. The charges have equal magnitude qqq flowing at a velocity vd\boldsymbol{v_d}vd​ (known as the drift velocity - velocity at which charges move when an external electric field is applied):

the current is equal to:

I=∬SJ⋅dA,I = \iint_S \boldsymbol{J} \cdot d\boldsymbol{A},I=∬S​J⋅dA,

where A\boldsymbol{A}A is the vector pointing perpendicular to the cross sectional area.

Suppose that in time ttt the charges move by x=vdtx = v_d tx=vd​t, then Q=qnAx=qnAvdtQ = q n A x = q n A v_d tQ=qnAx=qnAvd​t and the average current is equal to:

Iavg=Qt=qnAvd. \begin{align*} I_{avg} &= \frac{Q}{t} \\ &= q n A v_d. \end{align*} Iavg​​=tQ​=qnAvd​.​

Current density is the current per unit area:

J=nqvd,\boldsymbol{J} = nq \boldsymbol{v_d},J=nqvd​,

J\boldsymbol{J}J and vd\boldsymbol{v_d}vd​ point in the same direction for positive charges and in opposite direction for negative charges.

Next, we will find the drift velocity vd\boldsymbol{v_d}vd​. By Newton's second law, an electron experiences an acceleration equal to:

a=Feme=−eEme, \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{a} &= \frac{\boldsymbol{F_e}}{m_e} \\ &= -\frac{e \boldsymbol{E}}{m_e}, \end{align*} a​=me​Fe​​=−me​eE​,​

where e≈1.6⋅10−19 Ce \approx 1.6 \cdot 10^{-19}\ Ce≈1.6⋅10−19 C is the elementary charge.

Let the velocity of an electron immediately after a collision be vi\boldsymbol{v_i}vi​ the velocity of the same electron immediately before the next collision is equal to:

vf=vi+at=vi−eEmet. \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{v_f} &= \boldsymbol{v_i} + \boldsymbol{a}t \\ &= \boldsymbol{v_i} - \frac{e \boldsymbol{E}}{m_e}t. \end{align*} vf​​=vi​+at=vi​−me​eE​t.​

The average of vf\boldsymbol{v_f}vf​ is equal to:

⟨vf⟩=⟨vi⟩−eEme⟨t⟩,\langle\boldsymbol{v_f}\rangle = \langle\boldsymbol{v_i}\rangle - \frac{e \boldsymbol{E}}{m_e} \langle t\rangle,⟨vf​⟩=⟨vi​⟩−me​eE​⟨t⟩,

which is equal to the drift velocity.

In the absence of electric field the motion is completely random, where ⟨vi⟩=0\langle\boldsymbol{v_i}\rangle = \boldsymbol{0}⟨vi​⟩=0. If ⟨t⟩=τ\langle t\rangle = \tau⟨t⟩=τ, the average characteristics time between collisions, the drift velocity is equal to:

vd=−eEmeτ. \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{v_d} &= - \frac{e \boldsymbol{E}}{m_e} \tau. \end{align*} vd​​=−me​eE​τ.​

The current density is then equal to:

J=−nevd=ne2τmeE.\boldsymbol{J} = -n e \boldsymbol{v_d} = \frac{n e^2 \tau}{m_e} \boldsymbol{E}.J=−nevd​=me​ne2τ​E.

Note: J\boldsymbol{J}J and E\boldsymbol{E}E will always be in the same direction.

Ohm's Law 

The current density is usually expressed as:

J=σE,\boldsymbol{J} = \sigma \boldsymbol{E},J=σE,

where σ=ne2τme\sigma = \frac{n e^2 \tau}{m_e}σ=me​ne2τ​ is called the conductivity of the material. The above equation is known as the (microscopic) Ohm's law.

Suppose a cylindrical conductor of length lll with uniform electric field E\boldsymbol{E}E and with cross sectional area AAA:

The voltage is equal to:

U=Vb−Va=−∫abE⋅ds=El, \begin{align*} U &= V_b - V_a \\ &= -\int_a^b \boldsymbol{E} \cdot d\boldsymbol{s} \\ &= El, \end{align*} U​=Vb​−Va​=−∫ab​E⋅ds=El,​

implying E=UlE = \frac{U}{l}E=lU​. The magnitude of the current density is equal to:

J=σUl,J = \sigma \frac{U}{l},J=σlU​,

implying U=JlσU = \frac{J l}{\sigma}U=σJl​, where J=IAJ = \frac{I}{A}J=AI​:

U=IlAσ=RI,U = \frac{I l}{A \sigma} = RI,U=AσIl​=RI,

where R=lσAR = \frac{l}{\sigma A}R=σAl​ is the resistance of the conductor. The equation U=IRU = IRU=IR is the "macroscopic" version of the Ohm's law. The unit of resistance is ohm (Ω\OmegaΩ).

The resistivity ρ\rhoρ of a material is the reciprocal of conductivity:

ρ=1σ=mene2τ.\rho = \frac{1}{\sigma} = \frac{m_e}{n e^2 \tau}.ρ=σ1​=ne2τme​​.

Using E=UlE = \frac{U}{l}E=lU​ and J=IAJ = \frac{I}{A}J=AI​, the resistance can be derived:

J=Eρ  ⟹  ρ=EJ,ρ=UAIl=RAl,R=ρlA. \begin{align*} J &= \frac{E}{\rho} \implies \rho = \frac{E}{J}, \\ \rho &= \frac{UA}{Il} \\ &= \frac{RA}{l}, \\ R &= \frac{\rho l}{A}. \end{align*} JρR​=ρE​⟹ρ=JE​,=IlUA​=lRA​,=Aρl​.​

Power 

Consider a circuit with a battery and a resistor of resistance RRR:

The voltage between the points aaa and bbb is U=φb−φa>0U = \varphi_b - \varphi_a > 0U=φb​−φa​>0. If a charge qqq is moved from aaa to bbb through the battery, its electric potential energy is increased by:

ΔEp=qU.\Delta E_p = q U.ΔEp​=qU.

The charge qqq is unchanged upon returning to aaa. When the charge moves through the resistor, its potential energy is decreased. The rate of energy loss is equal to:

P=ΔEpt=qtU=IU, \begin{align*} P &= \frac{\Delta E_p}{t} \\ &= \frac{q}{t}U \\ &= IU, \end{align*} P​=tΔEp​​=tq​U=IU,​

this is the power supplied by the battery. Using U=IRU = IRU=IR, the equation can be rewritten:

P=I2R=U2R.P = I^2 R = \frac{U^2}{R}.P=I2R=RU2​.
