Magnetic Field

A bar magnet is one of the sources of magnetic field. The bar magnet consists of two poles, a north pole (N) and a south pole (S):

When holding two magnets close to each other, the like poles will repel each other while the opposite poles attract.

Magnetic poles always exist in pair. When a bar magnet is broken, two new bar magnets are obtained:

The electric field is the force per unit charge:

E=Feq,\boldsymbol{E} = \frac{\boldsymbol{F_e}}{q},E=qFe​​,

however, due to the absence of magnetic monopoles, the magnetic field B\boldsymbol{B}B must be defined differently.

To define it, consider a particle of charge qqq, moving at a velocity v\boldsymbol{v}v. Experimentally, it was found:

  1. The magnitude of the magnetic force FB\boldsymbol{F_B}FB​ is proportional to both vvv and qqq.
  2. The magnitude and direction of FB\boldsymbol{F_B}FB​ depends on v\boldsymbol{v}v and B\boldsymbol{B}B.
  3. FB\boldsymbol{F_B}FB​ vanishes when v\boldsymbol{v}v is parallel to B\boldsymbol{B}B. When v\boldsymbol{v}v and B\boldsymbol{B}B forms an angle θ\thetaθ, FB\boldsymbol{F_B}FB​ is perpendicular to a plane formed by them and the magnitude of FB\boldsymbol{F_B}FB​ is proportional to sin⁡θ\sin \thetasinθ.
  4. When the sign of qqq switches, the direction of FB\boldsymbol{F_B}FB​ reverses.

The observations can be summarized into:

FB=qv×B,FB=∣q∣vBsin⁡θ. \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{F_B} &= q\boldsymbol{v} \times \boldsymbol{B}, \\ F_B &= |q| v B \sin \theta. \end{align*} FB​FB​​=qv×B,=∣q∣vBsinθ.​

The unit of magnetic field is tesla (TTT). From B=FB∣q∣vB = \frac{F_B}{|q|v}B=∣q∣vFB​​, tesla is equal to:

T=NC m s−1=NA m.T = \frac{N}{C\ m\ s^{-1}} = \frac{N}{A\ m}.T=C m s−1N​=A mN​.

Another commonly used unit is gauss (GGG), where 1T=104G1 T = 10^4 G1T=104G.

Note that FB\boldsymbol{F_B}FB​ is always perpendicular to the plane formed by v\boldsymbol{v}v and B\boldsymbol{B}B and cannot change the particle's speed vvv. FB\boldsymbol{F_B}FB​ does no work on the particle:

dW=FB⋅ds=q(v×v)⋅B dt=0.dW = \boldsymbol{F_B} \cdot d\boldsymbol{s} = q(\boldsymbol{v} \times \boldsymbol{v}) \cdot \boldsymbol{B}\ dt = 0.dW=FB​⋅ds=q(v×v)⋅B dt=0.

However, the direction of v\boldsymbol{v}v can be changed by FB\boldsymbol{F_B}FB​.

Magnetic Force on a Current 

A charged particle moving at a speed v\boldsymbol{v}v experiences a magnetic force FB\boldsymbol{F_B}FB​. Electric current is a collection of moving charged particles, meaning a wire carrying current also experiences the magnetic force.

Consider a long straight wire suspended in the region between two magnetic poles. The magnetic field is illustrated by the black dots and flows out of the page.
