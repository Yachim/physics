Tensor Calculus Introduction

Tensor calculus is an extension of vector calculus to tensor fields. These tensor fields may vary over a manifold. Tensor calculus makes use of indices:

xμcontravariant,xμcovariant. \begin{align*} &x^{\mu} & &\textrm{contravariant}, \\ &x_{\mu} & &\textrm{covariant}. \end{align*} ​xμxμ​​​contravariant,covariant.​

A vector x\boldsymbol{x}x may be decomposed into a linear combination of components and basis vectors. In Cartesian coordinates, with components x, y, zx,\ y,\ zx, y, z and basis vectors i^, j^, k^\boldsymbol{\hat{i}},\ \boldsymbol{\hat{j}},\ \boldsymbol{\hat{k}}i^, j^​, k^, x\boldsymbol{x}x is equal to:

x=xi^+yj^+zk^.\boldsymbol{x} = x \boldsymbol{\hat{i}} + y \boldsymbol{\hat{j}} + z \boldsymbol{\hat{k}}.x=xi^+yj^​+zk^.

For a general vector x\boldsymbol{x}x with components xμx^{\mu}xμ and basis vectors eμ\boldsymbol{e_{\mu}}eμ​, the vector is equal to:

x=∑μxμeμ.\boldsymbol{x} = \sum_{\mu} x^{\mu} \boldsymbol{e_{\mu}}.x=μ∑​xμeμ​.

Sums like these appear a lot in tensor calculus, so we use the Einstein summation convention - when an index appears twice - once as upper (contravariant) and once as lower (covariant), it implies summation over the set:

x=xμeμ.\boldsymbol{x} = x^{\mu} \boldsymbol{e_{\mu}}.x=xμeμ​.

The vector x\boldsymbol{x}x is invariant - the vector remains unchanged after transformations (in this context coordinate transformations). However, the components xμx^{\mu}xμ and the basis vectors eμ\boldsymbol{e_{\mu}}eμ​ are variant.

Convariance and Contravariance
